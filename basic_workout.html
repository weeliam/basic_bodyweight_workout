The Workout

What are Bodyweight workouts?

Bodyweight workouts are exercises that use your body’s own weight as resistance. These types of exercises can be done anywhere. They are a great way to get a full-body workout without having to invest in expensive gym equipment. Exercises are an important part of any workout routine. They provide a way for individuals to strengthen and tone their bodies, improve their cardiovascular health, and increase their overall fitness level. Exercises can be tailored to individual needs, so that each person can focus on the areas of their body that need the most attention. Aerobic exercises can help with weight loss, while strength training exercises can help with building muscle and toning the body. Stretching exercises are also important for helping to reduce muscle soreness and maintain flexibility. It is important to include a variety of exercises in any workout routine in order to get the most benefit.

Benefits of Bodyweight Workouts

Bodyweight workouts have many benefits. They are a great way to get in shape and stay healthy. They are also convenient and can be done anywhere, anytime. Bodyweight exercises can also be modified to suit any fitness level, from beginner to advanced.

Warm up

Before any type of exercise, it’s important to warm up for at least 5-10 minutes. This allows time for the body to adjust to the intensity of the upcoming physical activity and helps to reduce the risk of injury. After warming up, it’s important to stretch in order to maintain flexibility.

Warm ups before exercise are essential for any type of physical activity. Doing warm up exercises helps to increase body temperature, blood flow and helps to prepare muscles for the upcoming exercise. Warm ups also help to reduce the risk of injury and improve performance.

Common warm up exercises include dynamic stretching, jogging, jumping jacks, mountain climbers and squats. These exercises help to increase flexibility and range of motion, reducing the risk of injury. Dynamic stretching is a great way to warm up as it helps to increase body temperature and prepare the muscles for exercise.

It’s important to take the time to warm up and stretch before any type of physical activity. Doing so will help to improve performance, reduce the risk of injury and maximize the benefits of the exercise.

Mobility exercises

Mobility exercises are movements that help to improve flexibility, range of motion, and overall joint health. Examples of mobility exercises as a warm up before training include dynamic stretching, foam rolling, and dynamic warm-up drills. Dynamic stretching involves actively moving joints and muscles through their full range of motion, while foam rolling is a form of self-myofascial release that helps to reduce tension and tightness in the muscles. Dynamic warm-up drills involve movements that mimic the activity you are about to perform and help to activate the muscles and increase blood flow to the area.

Example mobility warm up

Inch worm

-Warm up the shoulders and hips by flowing through this yoga-inspired mobility exercise.

-Begin in a high plank position with your abs engaged.

-Inhale as you lower your hips towards the ground and lift your chest up towards the sky, coming into upward dog. Your hips and knees should be off the mat.

-Exhale, and use your abs to pike your hips up towards the sky, coming into downward dog. Slowly return to high plank.

-Continue alternating between upward-facing dog and downward-facing dog for 5 reps.

Runner’s Lunge to Scissor Stretch

This mobility warm-up move stretches the hips and hamstrings while lubricating the hip, knee, and ankle joints.

Start in a high plank position.

Step your right foot outside of your right wrist. Your knee should be stacked over your ankle. Engage your abs and tent your fingers on the mat.

Inhale to gently lower your hips towards the ground, coming into a runner’s lunge.

Exhale to lift your hips up towards the sky and straighten out your legs, coming into a scissor stretch.

Move with your breath as you alternate between the two poses for 5 reps total.

Windshield Wiper

Gently warm up the hip joints and lower back with this easy exercise.

Sit on your mat with your knees bent and your feet out wider than your hips. Rest your hands on the floor behind you and lean back onto your palms, keeping your shoulders and elbows relaxed.

Exhale to lower your knees to the right.

Inhale to point your knees back up towards the ceiling and then exhale to lower them to the left.

Continue rocking your legs side-to-side for 10 reps in each direction.

Twisted Funky Pigeon

Warm up the hips, quads, lower back, and obliques with the feel-good hip opener.

Parallel your right shin to the top of your mat and bring your left leg back with the knee bent. Keep both feet flexed.

Tent your fingers in front of your right shin and lift your chest to lengthen your spine.

Walk your hands over to the right. If you can, lower down to your left forearm. Hold for 5 seconds.

Press back up to your hands and walk them in front of your right shin again.

Repeat this slow twist 5 times, then switch sides.

Dynamic Supine Twist

This mobility warm-up preps your spine, back, hips and shoulders for your workout.

Lie on your left side with your left leg straight your right knee bent. Bring your palms to touch with your arms straight out in front of you.

Take a deep inhale, then exhale to twist your right arm all the way open until the shoulder touches the ground.

Hold this supine twist for 5 seconds, then roll back to the starting position.

Repeat for 5 reps, then switch sides.

Three-Way Lunge

Take your lunge to the next level with this mobility warm up for your lower body and core.

Stand with your feet together and your hands clasped in front of your chest.Engage your abs.

Inhale to step your right foot forward to come into a forward lunge.Bend both knees towards 90 degrees and keep your torso upright.Drive through the right heel on an exhale to step your right foot back in line with your left.

Next, inhale to step your right foot straight out to the right to come into a side lunge, keeping your left leg straight. Exhale to step your right foot back to meet your left.

Lastly, inhale to step your right foot back at a 45-degree angle to come into a diagonal lunge. Keep the weight back in the right heel as you sit your hips back and down and keep your left leg straight. Make sure your knee stays in line with your toes. Exhale to step your right foot back up to meet your left.

Switch sides and perform all three lunges leading with the left leg.

Repeat the lunge routine on each side for a total of 3 times each.

Thoracic Bridge

This is the ultimate mobility warm-up. It opens the shoulders, hips, knees, and ankles while releasing tension in the hips and chest. It also prepares the core for a workout.

Sit with your knees bent, feet flat on the mat. Place your right hand down behind you with the fingers pointing away from your body.

Press into both feet and the right hand to lift your hips up. Then, reach your left arm across your body to come into a twist. Keep the palm facing down as you reach to feel a nice stretch in the upper back.

Hold here for 3 seconds, then gently lower back down. Switch sides, then continue alternating for a total of 5 reps per side.

The exercises

The basic bodyweight workout consists of several exercises that target different muscle groups in the body. These exercises do not require any equipment and can be done anywhere, making it a convenient workout for those who do not have access to a gym.

Some of the exercises included in the basic bodyweight workout are push-ups, squats, lunges, planks, and mountain climbers. Push-ups work the chest, shoulders, and triceps, while squats and lunges target the legs and glutes. Planks help strengthen the core muscles and mountain climbers provide a full-body workout that improves cardiovascular fitness.

Other exercises that are included to the basic bodyweight workout include burpees and bicycle crunches. These exercises increase the intensity of the workout and help burn more calories.

It is important to perform each exercise with proper form and technique to avoid injury and maximize the benefits of the workout. Beginners should start with fewer repetitions and gradually increase as they become comfortable with the exercises.

Push up

Push-ups are a great exercise for building upper body strength and endurance. They primarily work the muscles of the chest (pectoralis major and minor), triceps (back of the arms), and shoulders (deltoids). In addition, they also engage the muscles of the core, including the abdominals and lower back. Push-ups are a compound exercise, meaning they work multiple muscle groups at once, making them an efficient and effective exercise for building overall upper body strength.

How to do do the exercise

Start by getting into a plank position with your hands directly under your shoulders and your feet together.

Then, bend your elbows and lower your body until your chest almost touches the ground.

Push back up to the starting position and repeat!

Diamond push up

Diamond push ups mainly target the triceps, which are the muscles located at the back of your upper arm. They also engage the chest, shoulders, and core muscles to a lesser extent. The diamond shape hand position places more emphasis on the triceps as compared to standard push ups. By doing diamond push ups regularly, you can strengthen and tone your arms, improve your upper body stability, and enhance your overall fitness level.

How to do do the exercise

Start by getting into a standard pushup position, but with your hands slightly closer together so that your index fingers and thumbs form a diamond shape.

From there, lower yourself down until your chest almost touches the floor.

Then push back up.

Sit ups

Sit ups primarily target the rectus abdominis muscle, which is commonly referred to as the "six-pack muscle." In addition to the rectus abdominis, sit ups also engage the hip flexors, which are located in the front of the hips and assist in lifting the torso towards the knees. The obliques, which are located on either side of the rectus abdominis, are also activated during sit ups to help stabilize the torso and assist in rotation. Additionally, the erector spinae muscles of the lower back are used as stabilizers during the movement.

How to do do the exercise

Lie on the floor facing the ceiling with a slight bend in your knees and arms bent at the elbows and hands lightly touching your head by the ears.

Engage your core and lift your upper body so your elbows touch your knees.

Return to the start position.

Bicycle crunch

Bicycle crunches primarily target the rectus abdominis (the "six-pack" muscles) and the obliques (the muscles along the sides of your waist). However, they also engage other core muscles such as the transverse abdominis (the muscle that wraps around your midsection) and the hip flexors (the muscles that help lift your legs up towards your chest). In addition, bicycle crunches can also work your lower back muscles and glutes (depending on how you perform the exercise). Overall, bicycle crunches are a great way to strengthen and tone your core muscles.

How to do do the exercise

Lie on a mat with your lower back pressed into the mat.

Lift your knees up to form a 90-degree angle at your hip and knee.

Put your hands behind your head, then crunch and bring your right elbow towards your left knee while extending your right leg.

Switch sides by bringing in your left elbow towards your right knee while extending your left leg.

Dorsal raise

Dorsal raises, also known as back extensions, primarily target the muscles in the lower back. Specifically, they work the erector spinae muscles, which run along the length of the spine and help to maintain posture and support the back. Additionally, dorsal raises can also engage the gluteus maximus, hamstrings, and abdominal muscles to a lesser extent, depending on the execution and form of the exercise.

How to do do the exercise

Lie face down on the floor with your hands at your temples and your shoulders relaxed. Take a deep breath in, lifting your chest off the floor using your lower back muscles and glutes. Hold briefly, then, while breathing out, slowly lower back to the floor.

Make sure you don’t arch your neck back during the raise. Keep looking forwards, and don’t “throw” your shoulders back to aid the movement. Move slowly and with control at all times to maximise the benefits. The other key form tip is not to overextend your back. You don’t have to raise as far as you possibly can – just lift until you feel it in your lower back muscles, and then lower.

Keep your feet on the floor when first doing the move, and once you are adept at it you can increase the move’s difficulty by raising your feet. You can also stretch your arms out in front of you, or even hold a light weight, to intensify the challenge when doing the dorsal raise.

The plank

The plank is a great exercise that primarily targets the core muscles, including the rectus abdominis, transverse abdominis, and obliques. Additionally, it also works the muscles of the lower back, shoulders, and glutes. By holding the plank position, you can build strength and stability in your entire midsection, which can help improve your posture, reduce the risk of back pain, and enhance your overall athletic performance.

How to do do the exercise

To begin, lie flat with your belly facing the ground with your legs level and upper torso propped up by your forearms.

Lower your back and shoulder muscles, raise your buttocks off the ground.

Hold as long as you can (target for periods of 30 to 45 seconds ) and then relax.

Air squat

Air squats, also known as bodyweight squats, are a great exercise for targeting multiple muscle groups in the lower body. The main muscles used in air squats are the quadriceps, hamstrings, glutes, and calves.

How to do do the exercise

Start with feet a little bit wider than hip-width distance apart, weight in your heels, chest lifted.

As you inhale, lower down keeping the weight in your heels and chest lifted.

As you exhale, drive through your heels, squeezing your glutes and hamstring to stand back up.

Lunges

Lunges primarily target the muscles in the lower body, including the quadriceps, hamstrings, glutes, and calves. They also engage the core muscles, including the abdominals and lower back, to maintain balance and stability during the movement. Additionally, lunges can also help improve hip flexibility and range of motion.

How to do do the exercise

Stand with your feet hip-width apart, take a step forward with your right foot and then slowly bend both knees until your back knee is just above the floor.

Stand back up, take a step forward with your left foot and bend both knees until your back knee is just above the floor.

Repeat this forward movement for the entire duration of the set.

Burpees

Burpees are a full-body exercise that engages many muscles throughout the body. The primary muscles used in a burpee include the chest, triceps, shoulders, core, quadriceps, hamstrings, and glutes.

During the push-up portion of the burpee, the chest, triceps, and shoulders are heavily engaged. The jump portion of the burpee targets the quadriceps, hamstrings, and glutes. The core muscles are also heavily engaged throughout the entire exercise to maintain stability and control.

Overall, burpees are an excellent way to work multiple muscle groups at once and get a full-body workout in a short amount of time.

How to do do the exercise

Begin in a squat position with hands on the floor in front of you.

Kick your feet back to a pushup position.

Immediately return your feet to the squat position.

Leap up as high as possible from the squat position.

Tempo

Tempo for an exercise for strength is the speed at which you perform the exercise. Generally, exercises for strength are performed with a slower tempo, meaning that you would take longer to complete the movement. For example, if you are doing a squat, you would take 3-4 seconds to lower yourself down, pause for a second at the bottom, then take 2-3 seconds to come back up. This slower tempo helps to increase the time under tension, which helps to maximize the strength gains from the exercise.

Reps and sets

Reps

Definition of Reps

Reps = the number of times you perform a given exercise

Sets

Definition of Sets

Sets = the number of cycles of reps you complete

Reps and sets are an important part of any bodyweight workout. Reps, or repetitions, refer to the number of times you perform a certain exercise. Sets refer to the number of times you perform a group of exercises. For example, if you are doing a bodyweight workout that includes 10 push-ups, you would do 10 reps of push-ups and that would be one set.

Reps and sets are important for bodyweight workouts because they help you build strength and endurance. By doing multiple sets of an exercise, you are able to increase your muscular endurance and build strength. Doing multiple reps of an exercise also helps you to increase your muscular endurance and build strength.

When doing bodyweight workouts, it is important to make sure that you are doing the right number of reps and sets for your fitness level. If you are a beginner, it is best to start with fewer reps and sets and gradually increase as you get stronger. If you are more advanced, you can increase the number of reps and sets to challenge yourself and help you reach your fitness goals.

Example reps and sets:

  • - Push-up: 3 sets of 10 reps, rest 30 seconds in between sets
  • - Diamond Push-ups: 3 sets of 8 reps, rest 30 seconds in between sets
  • - Sit up: 3 sets of 12 reps, rest 30 seconds in between sets
  • - Bicycle crunches: 3 sets of 12 reps, rest 30 seconds in between sets
  • - Plank: 3 sets of 30 secs, rest 30 seconds in between sets
  • - Dorsal raise: 3 sets of 12 reps, rest 30 seconds in between sets
  • - Squats: 3 sets of 12 reps, rest 30 seconds in between sets
  • - Lunges: 3 sets of 12 reps (each leg), rest 30 seconds in between sets
  • - Burpees: 3 sets of 12 reps, rest 30 seconds in between sets

Timed circuit

A timed circuit with a set split of 10 to 40 seconds work and 5 to 20 seconds rest between exercises with a 1 to 2 minutes rest between sets is a type of high-intensity interval training (HIIT). This type of workout involves alternating short bursts of intense exercise with short periods of rest or low-intensity exercise. The goal is to push the body to its maximum capacity for a short period of time. HIIT workouts are an effective way to burn fat and build muscle in a short amount of time.

Example timed circuit:

  • - Push-up (1 minute)
  • - Squat (1 minute)
  • - Situp (1 minute)
  • - Diamond push-up (1 minute)
  • - Bicycle crunches (1 minute)
  • - Lunges (1 minute)
  • - Dorsal raises (1 minute)
  • - Burpees (1 minute)
  • - Plank (1 minute)

Repeat for 3-5 rounds.

These are just a few examples of the different types of bodyweight workouts you can do. Remember to start slow and gradually increase the intensity as you get stronger. And don't forget to stretch before and after your workouts to prevent injury!

Cooldown

Exercising is a great way to stay fit and healthy, but it’s important to make sure that you’re giving your body enough time to rest and recover. Cooldown after exercise is an important part of the process, as it helps to bring your heart rate and breathing rate back to normal and prevent any soreness or injuries.

Cooldown typically involves five to ten minutes of stretching and light aerobic activity, such as walking or jogging. This can help to restore your body to its pre-exercise state and reduce lactic acid buildup. Stretching can also help to reduce tightness in the muscles, improve range of motion, and reduce the risk of injury.

Some people also like to practice deep breathing or meditation during their cooldown period, as these activities can help to reduce stress and promote relaxation. Additionally, drinking plenty of water and having a healthy snack can help to replenish the nutrients that have been lost during exercise.

Including cooldown in your exercise routine can be a great way to help your body recover and prevent any injuries. Taking the time to properly cool down after exercising can help to ensure that your body is able to perform at its best.

Cooldown stretches

Cooldown stretches after training are stretches that are done at the end of a workout to help the body recover from the physical exertion and to reduce the risk of injury. They are typically held for 20 - 30 seconds and focus on the muscles used during the workout. Examples of cooldown stretches include calf stretches, hamstring stretches, chest stretches, and shoulder stretches.

Hamstrings and calves stretch

Do: 30 sec per side

Hip flexors stretch

How to do do the exercise

Do: 30 sec per side

Start in a kneeling position on your mat. Release your left leg and take one large step forwards so that you are in a low lunge position, ensuring that your left knee is not further forward than your toes. If it is, then you will need to take a bigger step forward.

Keeping your torso upright, push your hips forwards so that you feel a stretch along the front of your right leg.

Hold this position. Each time that you exhale, use your abdominals to tuck your pubic bone towards your belly button to increase the stretch. Repeat this stretch on the other side.

Do: 30 sec per side

Start in the centre of your mat on all fours, ensuring that your shoulders are stacked over your hands and your hips are over your knees, toes untucked.

Summary

In summary if you're looking to get in shape but don't have access to a gym or equipment, a basic bodyweight workout is a great option. By simply using your own body weight as resistance, you can build strength and improve your overall fitness.

To get started, plan to do a basic bodyweight workout three times a week, making sure you have at least a day of rest in between each workout. This will give your muscles time to recover and prevent injury.

When designing your workout, focus on exercises that target multiple muscle groups at once. Some examples include push-ups, squats, lunges, planks, and burpees. These exercises will work your entire body and help you get the most out of your workout.

Start with a warm-up to get your heart rate up and your muscles ready for exercise. This could include jogging in place, jumping jacks, or even a few minutes of light stretching.

Next, move on to your main workout. Aim to do three sets of each exercise, with 10-12 reps per set. If you're just starting out, you may want to start with fewer reps and work your way up as you get stronger.

After your main workout, finish with a cool-down to help your body recover. This could include stretching, foam rolling, or even a few minutes of light yoga.

Remember to listen to your body and take breaks as needed. If an exercise feels too challenging, modify it or take a break. And don't forget to stay hydrated throughout your workout.

By doing a basic bodyweight workout three times a week, you can build strength, improve your fitness, and feel great. With a little consistency and dedication, you'll be on your way to a healthier, happier you.
